Introduction

Digital platforms have reshaped how people communicate in daily life: simple expressions, emoji cues, and flexible code choice. In contrast, face-to-face (f2f) interaction relies on the tone, gesture, and shared context while doing the pragmatic work. Studies regarding English email by Hong Kong students indicate that the digital environment has fostered a hybrid style that differs from traditional face-to-face communication, and one can expect the appearance of Cantonese particles and other colloquial features (James, 2001). This is particularly obvious for various languages users since online texts compensate for the loss of f2f signals- intonation, particles, and eye contact- with orthographic punctuation and emojis, while f2f talk relies on paralinguistic resources and particles in expressing stance and politeness. This site relies on LLMs to investigate differences in language use online and f2f.

The study in this website compares the online and offline communication modalities of language uses:

1. What difference is there in emoji use, sentence-final particles, code choice, and formality between online and face-to-face responses?

2. When prompted similarly, do LLM responses align more with an online or offline human style?

3. Where do LLMs diverge? What patterns do such divergences show (e.g., over-formality, under-emoji use)?
